# The Solar System

## Overview
The Solar System consists of the Sun and the objects that orbit it, including eight planets,
their moons, dwarf planets, asteroids, and comets. It formed approximately 4.6 billion years ago
from the gravitational collapse of a giant molecular cloud.

## The Sun
The Sun is a G-type main-sequence star at the center of the Solar System. It accounts for
about 99.86% of the total mass of the Solar System. The Sun's energy is produced by nuclear
fusion of hydrogen into helium in its core. The surface temperature is approximately 5,500°C.

## The Inner Planets

### Mercury
Mercury is the smallest planet and the closest to the Sun. It has no atmosphere to retain heat,
resulting in extreme temperature variations: 430°C during the day and -180°C at night.
Mercury completes an orbit around the Sun in just 88 Earth days.

### Venus
Venus is the second planet from the Sun and the hottest planet in the Solar System, with surface
temperatures reaching 465°C due to a runaway greenhouse effect. Its atmosphere is composed
mostly of carbon dioxide. A day on Venus (243 Earth days) is longer than its year (225 Earth days).

### Earth
Earth is the third planet and the only known astronomical object to harbor life. It has one
natural satellite, the Moon. Earth's atmosphere protects life by absorbing ultraviolet solar
radiation, warming the surface through heat retention (greenhouse effect), and reducing
temperature extremes between day and night.

### Mars
Mars is the fourth planet and the second-smallest in the Solar System. It has two small moons,
Phobos and Deimos. Mars has the tallest volcano in the Solar System, Olympus Mons (21 km high),
and the longest valley system, Valles Marineris. Evidence suggests liquid water once flowed on Mars.

## The Outer Planets

### Jupiter
Jupiter is the fifth planet and the largest in the Solar System — more than twice as massive as
all other planets combined. It is a gas giant with no solid surface. The Great Red Spot is a
storm larger than Earth that has been ongoing for at least 350 years. Jupiter has 95 known moons,
including Europa (which may harbor a subsurface ocean), Ganymede, Io, and Callisto.

### Saturn
Saturn is the sixth planet and is famous for its stunning ring system made of ice and rock
particles. It is the least dense planet — it would float on water. Saturn has 146 known moons,
with Titan being the largest and having a thick nitrogen atmosphere.

### Uranus
Uranus is the seventh planet and an ice giant. It rotates on its side, with an axial tilt of
97.77 degrees, likely the result of a massive collision early in its history. Uranus has a faint
ring system and 27 known moons, named after characters from Shakespeare and Alexander Pope.

### Neptune
Neptune is the eighth and farthest known planet. It is an ice giant with the strongest winds
in the Solar System, reaching 2,100 km/h. Neptune has 16 known moons; the largest, Triton,
orbits in a retrograde direction, suggesting it was captured from the Kuiper Belt.

## Dwarf Planets
Dwarf planets are celestial bodies that orbit the Sun but haven't cleared their orbital
neighborhood. The five recognized dwarf planets are: Pluto, Eris, Haumea, Makemake, and Ceres.
Pluto was reclassified from a planet to a dwarf planet in 2006 by the International Astronomical
Union.

## The Kuiper Belt and Oort Cloud
The Kuiper Belt is a region beyond Neptune filled with icy objects. The Oort Cloud is a
theoretical shell of icy bodies surrounding the Solar System at distances up to 100,000 AU
from the Sun. Long-period comets originate from the Oort Cloud.

## Key Facts
- Distance from Sun to Earth: ~150 million km (1 AU)
- Age of Solar System: ~4.6 billion years
- Number of planets: 8
- Number of dwarf planets (recognized): 5
- Largest planet: Jupiter
- Smallest planet: Mercury
- Only planet with known life: Earth
